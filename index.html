4.1 — What is Selective Attention?

Selective attention is the cognitive process by which the brain prioritizes certain stimuli from the environment while filtering out irrelevant information. This mechanism is crucial for effective learning, as it determines which information makes it into working memory.

"We don't see things as they are; we see them as we are. The brain constructs reality from a flood of sensory input, choosing what matters most at any given moment."

The attentional spotlight metaphor suggests that our focus operates like a beam of light — where the light shines, processing is enhanced; elsewhere, it is suppressed. Research using eye-tracking has confirmed that where the eyes go, attention follows.

4.2 — The Cognitive Load Model

Proposed by John Sweller in 1988, Cognitive Load Theory argues that working memory has a strictly limited capacity. When this capacity is exceeded — through complex material, distractions, or emotional stress — learning degrades significantly.

Total Load = Intrinsic Load + Extraneous Load + Germane Load

Intrinsic load arises from the complexity of the material itself. Extraneous load comes from poorly designed instruction or distracting environments. Germane load is the productive effort devoted to forming schemas — the building blocks of long-term memory.

4.3 — Focus & Neurodiversity

Students with ADHD often experience fluctuating attentional states, making sustained focus particularly challenging. Research indicates that environmental cues — visual anchors, rhythmic stimuli, or gentle movement — can act as focus regulators, especially during independent study.

For students on the autism spectrum, hyperfocus episodes may alternate with sudden disengagement. Predictable, low-intensity sensory feedback is often more effective than abrupt interruptions.

Gentle, non-intrusive cues respect the learner's cognitive autonomy while quietly redirecting attention before a full distraction cascade begins.

4.4 — Memory Consolidation

Information moves from working memory to long-term memory through a process called consolidation, which is heavily dependent on focus quality during encoding. Distributed practice (spaced repetition) and active recall consistently outperform passive re-reading.

The spacing effect demonstrates that learning sessions spread over time produce stronger retention than a single massed session — a core principle behind modern flashcard systems like Anki.
